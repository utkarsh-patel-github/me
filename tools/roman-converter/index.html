Roman Numeral System

Roman numerals are a numeral system that originated in ancient Rome. Numbers are represented by combinations of letters from the Latin alphabet.

Roman Numeral Rules:

  • Numerals are generally written largest to smallest from left to right.
  • When a smaller value precedes a larger value, the smaller value is subtracted from the larger value.
  • Examples of subtractive notation: IV = 4, IX = 9, XL = 40, XC = 90, CD = 400, CM = 900.
  • The same numeral cannot be used more than three times in succession (e.g., III = 3, but IIII is not valid).

FAQ

What is the largest number I can convert?

Standard Roman numerals can represent numbers from 1 to 3,999. Beyond that, special notation with overlines would be needed, which isn't commonly used today. Our converter supports numbers within the 1-3,999 range.

Why can't I convert the number 0?

The ancient Romans didn't have a symbol for zero. The concept of zero as a number came from Indian mathematics and was later adopted in Europe through Arabic numerals. Roman numerals only represent positive integers.

Are Roman numerals still used today?

Yes, Roman numerals are still used in several contexts: clock faces, book chapters and volumes, movie sequels, major sporting events like the Olympics and Super Bowl, outlines, and monarchs' names (like Elizabeth II).

Can Roman numerals represent fractions?

Standard Roman numerals cannot represent fractions or decimal values. The Romans did have separate symbols for fractions in certain contexts (particularly for weights and measures), but these aren't part of the standard Roman numeral system we use today.
